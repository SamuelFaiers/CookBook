Seasoning

Seasoning is added to a dish to enhance its flavour. Salt, spices and herbs are all clasified as seasoning. The only thing worse than too little seasoning is too much, so understanding how to season is important.

How to Season

Most recipes call for seasoning to taste. The best way to approach this is to add an initial pinch to the dish and taste it. If it tastes fine as is, then no more seasoning is needed, if it seems a bit bland, add another pinch and repeat.

Salt

Salt is the most common form of seasoning you will use. This most important difference between salts is the shape of their crystals. Fine salts have very small crystals giving it sand like consistency, which makes it difficult to handle. Flaky salts are easier to handle due to having larger crystals, however, they are more expensive.

Fine Salt

This disolves quickly making it best for moist-heat cooking and baking.

Flake Salt

Easier to handle. Best for dry-heat cooking excluding baking.

The larger irregular crystals of flake salt also make it less compact than fine salt. What this means is that is that flake salt can have up to half the volume of fine salt. This is an important distinction to make for recipes that call for specific measurements. In these cases, use fine salt unless stated otherwise.

Salt is Salt!

Ultimately, there is no difference in flavour between different varieties of salt. Those like Himalayan pink salt add to nothing but the appearance and cost of your dish. They should therefore be used to garnish (decorate) when you want to show off.

Pepper

Black pepper is the second most used form of seasoning. Freshly ground black pepper is always best. In which case, you will need a pepper mill with whole pepper corns rather than already ground pepper.

Alternatively, most supermarkets sell pepper corns in disposable mills. That said a pepper mill is a better long term investment.
