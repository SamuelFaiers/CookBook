Dry-heat Cooking

Dry-heat cooking encompasses all methods of cooking that do not utilise water. The goal of these methods is to brown food through the Maillard reaction, which gives food a greater depth of flavour. This reaction can only be achieved at temperatures above 140°C.

Baking

Food is baked by hot dry air, most often in an oven. This usually results in a browned crust yet soft interior.

Baking often requires very specific measurements. So it is important to have the right equipment and to follow recipes to the dot.

Roasting

Roasting is similar to baking. It is best used for large or whole pieces of meat along with root and bulb vegetables.

● 95°C to 160°C: Large or whole pieces of meat. This minimizes moisture loss and leads to a tenderer result.

● 180°C to 220°C: Tender cuts. This will cook quickly to minimize moisture loss and brown the outside.

Griddling

Griddling is ideal for quickly cooking smaller cuts of meat. This method is most often seen in barbeques, but griddled pans can achieve the same result.

The defining feature of griddled food is the markings, caused by the wire grids or raised edges of griddling cookware. This is harder to achieve with stainless steel griddles, so cast-iron is best.

Grilling

Grills usually come as a separate part of an oven, placed below the hob. Food is placed directly under a heating element on a wire rack within another tray to catch any excess juices. It is essentially the reverse of griddling.

Grilling cooks food very quickly, so be sure to keep close eye on your food when it is cooking.
